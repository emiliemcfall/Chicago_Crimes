Chicago Crime Analysis

“Is it really as bad as all the news say it is?”, said Mary Pattillo, Northwestern University sociologist and IPR associate in a Union Club League of Chicago’s event. Mary centered her discussion around the biggest problem Chicago faces today: crime and violence. In the Chicago news today, we constantly hear about theft and homicides, and police and anti-crime organizations efforts to address these issues. While it is remarkable that Chicago’s murder rate has fallen 20% in the last year, we found that homicides are not actually the biggest amount of sub-crimes in Chicago.

Since we all have personal connections with Chicago, we want to help the city of Chicago and anti-crime organizations with their efforts to reduce crime. Through our rigorous analysis of Chicago crime-based datasets, we have broken down Chicago crime data and identified important trends to help reduce the crime rates for the biggest sub-crimes.

Throughout this website, we will let our data lead the story and convince your respective organizations to reconsider your resource allocation towards theft crime.

We have spent so much time conducting analyses on Chicago data that we wanted to give you all context on what background information we used to reach our conclusions. Here we have created a zoomable map where you can click on a police district of Chicago and learn more about demographics that we felt were relevant to set the scene of Chicago.

Sub-Crime Trends From 2011-2018

Arrest/No Arrest Ratio

In the above line chart the trend for the top 4 subcategories of theft is shown. It is clearly visible how all crimes have decreased from 2008 to 2011, but from around 2011 the trend has stagnated - especially for Retail Theft which even increased a bit again from around 2014.

In the plot to the right we have visualised the arrest-no arrest ratio for each of these 4 sub crimes. Try to filter for “From Building” and you can see how the ratio between arrest and no arrest marginally decreases. Try instead to filter for “Retail Theft” and see how different the ratio is for this crime type.

Is it possible to see differences in the pattern of these two crimes? Is the difference in arrrest- no arrest due to locational differences? Let us see the points on a map.

Map of Chicago

From the above plot it is possible to detect different patterns for the crime types. It is not obvious that there are differences in the locational patterns of the two crimetypes. Instead, we do see a clear pattern of differences between the seasons. Try to use the filters to change from Summer to Winter and see how big of a difference there is.

Is this an actual difference or is it just an optical illusion? We will examine this with a box plot in the next section.

Seasonal Impact on Theft Crime

The same pattern is definitely detected from the boxplots. It is seen that the median line of Winter is below the lower interquartile range of both Fall and Spring and therefore likely to be different from these two seasons. Further to this it is seen that the entire box of Winter is below the entire box of Summer and therefore there is a difference between these two seasons.

It is known that the Winters i Chicago can get very cold with temperatures all the way down to -20 degrees, which could be an explanation of the differences we see.
